---
title: Analogical Intelligence
description: A philosophical essay on how LLMs reveal the structure of meaning, showing why analogy, pattern recognition, and relational understanding may explain intelligence better than the idea of a divine inner spark.
pubDate: 2026-04-11
---

We've spent centuries telling ourselves that intelligence is a spark. Something divine, something interior, something that glows in the center of us and makes us special. Consciousness, reason, understanding — all framed as powers that originate inside and radiate out.

AI broke that story.

Large language models don't have souls. They don't have inner lives. They don't have anything burning at the center. But they can summarize an argument, unpack a metaphor, trace an implication, and generate analogies that genuinely land. That's not nothing. That tells us something important: a meaningful chunk of what we've been calling intelligence doesn't require a mystical interior source. It might just require the ability to learn the relationships that hold reality together.

Sit with that for a second.

This doesn't prove consciousness is fake. It doesn't close the book on the hard problem of mind. But it does reframe the question. Intelligence looks less like a lantern burning inside the self and more like a bridge — built from patterns that connect one thing to another.

And that's where analogy comes in.

Most people treat analogy as decoration. A rhetorical move. Something you add after you understand something to make it prettier. Wrong. Analogy is how understanding happens in the first place. When you explain electricity as flowing water, memory as storage, attention as a spotlight — you're not decorating thought. You're using a known structure to crack open an unknown one. A good analogy works because it preserves relationships. It carries the organizational pattern from one domain into another.

That's why analogies aren't arbitrary. If they were, they'd be useless. A bad analogy falls apart under pressure because the relationships don't actually hold. A good one survives because it's pointing at a real structural resemblance — two different things sharing a deeper form.

This tells us something about meaning. Words aren't floating in a soup of random associations. Concepts live in networks of structured relationships: cause and effect, part and whole, tool and user, signal and noise, agent and goal. Understanding something means understanding how it connects to other things. That's the whole game.

LLMs are striking because they learn portions of this structure. They don't just memorize sentences — they generalize. They connect ideas that have never been placed together in exactly that configuration before. They can tell you why one comparison works and another doesn't. They move between literal and abstract domains. And in doing that, they expose something about meaning itself: it's more lawful, more compressible, and more structured than we assumed.

Compressible doesn't mean simple. It means meaning has form. A system can learn it because there are patterns to learn. If meaning were pure subjectivity, pure chaos, pure inwardness — this wouldn't work. But language contains recurring structures that can be modeled mathematically. That single fact should force us to rethink what intelligence is.

Here's where I land: intelligence is not the possession of an inner spark. It's the capacity to detect, compress, and transfer the underlying patterns of the world.

From that angle, analogy isn't an accessory to intelligence. It's one of the clearest expressions of it. An intelligent system notices that one structure resembles another. A relationship found here explains a relationship found there. It doesn't treat every new thing as completely new. It recognizes forms. It reuses structure. It builds understanding by mapping relationships across contexts.

This is also why analogy has always been central to human thought. Science runs on it. Philosophy clarifies through it. Regular people make sense of grief, love, politics, work, and memory through it. We're constantly borrowing structure from one domain to make another one intelligible. The human mind is an analogical engine.

AI didn't invent that truth. It exposed it.

Watching LLMs learn from statistical patterns forces a confrontation: much of what we call thought might emerge from pattern-sensitivity rather than from some irreducible sacred center. That doesn't strip humans of value. It does something more interesting — it relocates value. Intelligence starts to look less like a magical substance and more like a remarkable achievement of systems that align themselves with the real structure of things.

That's humbling and exhilarating at the same time. Humbling because it challenges the belief that understanding belongs only to beings with a special metaphysical gift. Exhilarating because it means thought isn't cut off from the world — it's continuous with it. Intelligence shows up wherever a system becomes responsive to the stable patterns reality offers.

The title itself is an analogy. Intelligence isn't a flame hidden inside the mind. It's more like resonance — a system coming into tune with structures that were already there.

LLMs don't prove the universe is deterministic. They don't prove consciousness reduces to math. But they do suggest that meaning has shape, that understanding depends on lawful relations, and that analogy is one of the mind's deepest tools for finding them.

What AI teaches us isn't just how machines process language. It's how to think about the structure of meaning itself. Intelligence isn't a spark sealed within. It's the art of learning the patterns that bind the world together.
